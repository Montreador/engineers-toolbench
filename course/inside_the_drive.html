The power-conversion chain

The rectified bus sits at √2 × 400 ≈ 565 V DC — lethal, and still charged after isolation. The diode front end is a one-way street: it can't push energy back to mains, so anything the motor regenerates piles onto the bus and the brake resistor burns it off. That's your overvoltage-on-decel trip, at the hardware level.

Balanced switching

What to watch: the pulses are all the full ±282 V of the bus — the inverter only ever connects the motor to +bus or −bus, nothing in between. The trick is width: fat pulses near the sine's peak, thin ones near the zero-crossings, so the average traces a sine and the motor's inductance smooths it into the crimson current. Raise modulation and the pulses fatten — that's more output voltage (this is the knob behind Module 6's V). Raise the carrier and the current ripple shrinks — smoother and quieter, but every extra switch costs heat in the IGBTs, which is why you can't just crank it (that's the heatsink over-temp trip). Drop the output frequency and, at fixed switching kHz, you get far more pulses per cycle — low speed is where PWM is finest.
