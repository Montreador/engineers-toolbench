The edge travels, hits the motor, and bounces back

The motor end is nearly an open circuit to a fast edge, so the wave reflects and adds to itself — the terminal can swing toward twice the bus voltage before the reflection races back and rings it down.

What the first winding turn sees

Within a standard motor's insulation.

Run the experiment: start with a fast 100 ns IGBT and drag cable length up. Below the critical length the terminal barely overshoots; past it the peak climbs to nearly 2× the bus — around 1050 V — and every one of those spikes hammers the first turn of the winding. That's why inverter-duty motors (reinforced insulation, ~1600 V rated) exist, and why a standard motor on a long run fails early. Now the counterintuitive part: make the IGBT faster (lower ns) and it gets worse — better switches, harsher on the motor. The fixes: a dV/dt filter slows the edge so the overshoot shrinks, and a sine filter removes the PWM entirely — a real sinewave, any cable length, standard motor, silent.
